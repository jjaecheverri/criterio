Office-to-Residential Conversion Is Structurally Constrained

Despite favorable headlines and policy support, fewer than 1% of vacant office buildings are physically or financially suitable for residential conversion — the structural and financial barriers are more binding than the political will.

Office Conversion Reality Check

▸ US office vacancy rate: 20.1% (Q4 2025) — highest since 1990s S&L crisis

▸ Estimated structurally convertible share: 10-15% of vacant stock (CBRE, JLL estimates)

▸ Average conversion cost: $150-500/sq ft depending on building vintage and market

▸ Completed conversions 2020-2025: ~50,000 units nationwide — 0.3% of stated conversion potential

▸ NYC conversions enabled by 2023 zoning reform: 485 buildings identified; 23 active conversion projects as of Q1 2026

The office-to-residential narrative has attracted enormous policy attention because it appears to solve two problems simultaneously: reducing commercial vacancy and adding housing supply. The Biden administration allocated $35 billion in financing support for conversions; cities from New York to Chicago to San Francisco relaxed zoning restrictions; and developers floated ambitious plans. The result, as of early 2026, is a modest trickle of completions against a backdrop of structural barriers that no policy instrument has meaningfully addressed. The gap between the conversion opportunity as described in press releases and the conversion opportunity as experienced by developers is vast and largely unbridgeable with current economics.

The physical constraints are the binding factor. The ideal conversion candidate is a building with a small floor plate — ideally under 10,000 square feet — with windows on all four sides, a floor-to-floor height above 12 feet, and a core-to-window depth of no more than 30 feet to allow natural light penetration. Most of the office vacancy in the US is concentrated in large-plate Class B and C towers from the 1970s-1990s with floor plates of 20,000-40,000 square feet, core-to-window depths of 50-70 feet, and floor-to-floor heights of 9-11 feet. These buildings cannot be converted without hollowing out the floor plate — an engineering intervention that adds $100-200/sq ft to already expensive conversion costs. Industry analysts estimate 85-90% of vacant office inventory fails one or more of these physical screens.

The Financial Gap

Even among the small universe of physically suitable buildings, the financial math rarely closes without substantial public subsidy. Converting a Class B office building in a secondary market costs $180-250/sq ft; the resulting residential units need to achieve $2.50-3.50/sq ft/month in rent to justify the all-in development cost. In markets where office vacancy is highest — Dallas, Houston, Chicago suburban, Philadelphia — residential rents frequently do not support those returns without below-market financing, tax abatements, or historic tax credits. New York, Washington DC, and Boston have the rental rates to support conversion economics, but also the most expensive construction costs and the most constrained zoning. The cities most willing to incentivize conversions are often the ones least able to make the economics work on their own.

Office-to-residential conversion will contribute meaningfully to housing supply in perhaps 15-20 markets nationally where the physical stock, rental rates, and policy environment align. In the broader universe of 20%+ vacancy markets, it is a boutique strategy that addresses perhaps 5-10% of the structural oversupply. The US does not have an office-to-housing problem. It has an office demolition and land recycling problem — and that process is slower, less glamorous, and less politically convenient to discuss.

Institutional Single-Family Rental Is Reshaping Starter Home Markets

Institutional investors own 3-5% of single-family rental homes nationally but 15-25% in specific Sun Belt submarkets, creating measurable price effects and supply constraints for first-time buyers in the most affected markets.

Institutional SFR Market Data

▸ Total US SFR homes: ~16 million; institutional-owned (>1,000 units): ~600,000-750,000 (3-5%)

▸ Atlanta metro institutional SFR share: estimated 15-20% of rental stock in zip codes 30340-30360

▸ Charlotte, Phoenix, Dallas top markets: 10-18% institutional share in specific zip codes (Harvard JCHS 2024)

▸ Invitation Homes portfolio: 85,000+ homes; AMH: 59,000+; Progress Residential: 85,000+

▸ Academic finding: 1% increase in institutional ownership associated with 1.2-2.1% home price increase in affected zip codes (Fed Reserve Atlanta, 2023)

The debate about institutional single-family rental has been poorly served by national statistics. At 3-5% of the total single-family rental universe, institutional owners appear to be a minor market actor — and at the national level, they are. But housing markets do not clear nationally; they clear locally, at the zip code level, and in the specific price tiers and geographies where these investors have concentrated their activity, the market impact is material and measurable. The relevant unit of analysis is not "all US homes" but "homes priced $180,000-$350,000 in zip codes within 30 miles of Atlanta, Phoenix, and Charlotte CBDs" — and in that more precise market definition, institutional ownership has reached levels that meaningfully affect price discovery.

The mechanism is not primarily that institutions outbid first-time buyers at the margin — though that does occur. The more durable effect is that institutional investors acquire homes, convert them to rentals, and permanently remove them from the for-sale inventory. A home that is owned by Invitation Homes is a home that is not available for a family to purchase for the duration of institutional ownership, which given the economics of scale and platform typically means 10-20 years. Concentrated acquisition in specific price tiers has effectively converted a meaningful share of what would have been the starter-home inventory into institutionally managed rental stock. The for-sale market in affected zip codes has become structurally thinner.

Home price increase associated with each 1% increase in institutional ownership in affected zip codes — Federal Reserve Atlanta analysis

The policy response — legislative restrictions on institutional SFR ownership in several states — is unlikely to reverse the accumulated inventory transfer but may slow future concentration. The more consequential policy lever is zoning reform to enable new construction of starter homes, which addresses the supply shortage that made institutional SFR economics attractive in the first place. Attacking the symptom (institutional ownership) without addressing the cause (supply constraints) is analytically weak policy.

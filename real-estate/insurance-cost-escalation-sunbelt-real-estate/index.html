Insurance Cost Escalation Is Repricing Sunbelt Real Estate

Property insurance premiums in Florida, Texas, and Louisiana have increased 40-80% since 2020, fundamentally altering cap rates and investor underwriting assumptions across the Sun Belt.

Insurance Cost Escalation — Key Data

▸ Florida homeowner insurance: avg premium up 42% since 2020, now $6,000+/yr in coastal counties

▸ 7 major insurers have exited Florida market since 2022, leaving Citizens Property Insurance as insurer of last resort with 1.2M policies

▸ Texas coastal premiums: up 60-80% since 2021 in Harris and Galveston counties

▸ Commercial property insurance: nationwide up 15-20% in 2024, 25%+ in Sunbelt markets

▸ Louisiana average premium: $2,909/yr — highest in the US at $1,000+ above national average

The insurance market for Sunbelt real estate has undergone a structural repricing that is not cyclical but reflects reinsurance markets absorbing the true actuarial cost of climate risk. Between 2017 and 2023, insured losses from natural catastrophes averaged $120 billion annually — more than double the 2000-2010 average — and reinsurers have responded by dramatically raising the cost of risk transfer. For property owners and investors in Florida, Texas, and Louisiana, this repricing is not a temporary dislocation; it is the permanent new equilibrium, and it is working its way into cap rates, NOI, and asset values with a lag that the market has not yet fully priced.

The mechanism is direct and compounding. A multifamily property in coastal Florida that budgeted $800/unit/year for insurance in 2020 is now paying $1,400-1,800 in comparable markets. That delta — $600-1,000/unit — flows directly to operating expenses, reducing NOI by 8-15% holding rents constant. At a 5.5% cap rate, every $100,000 reduction in annual NOI translates to $1.8 million in lost asset value. For a 200-unit property that has seen insurance costs rise $1,000/unit annually, the implied value impairment approaches $3.6 million before accounting for any increase in the discount rate itself. Investors who underwrote these properties at 2020 insurance costs are facing a reckoning that many have not yet fully marked to market.

Investor Underwriting Is Changing

Sophisticated institutional buyers are now stress-testing insurance costs at 2-3x current levels in their underwriting models for Sunbelt acquisitions — a practice that would have seemed paranoid five years ago but is now table stakes. Some are requiring sellers to provide three years of actual insurance invoices rather than relying on proforma assumptions. The bid-ask spread in coastal Florida commercial real estate widened significantly in 2024-2025 as buyers applied higher insurance costs and sellers held to 2022-vintage valuations. The resolution of this spread will be downward price adjustment in the most exposed markets.

The Sunbelt insurance crisis is not primarily a regulatory failure — it is the market correcting for decades of underpriced risk. The question for real estate investors is not whether insurance costs will normalize, but whether NOI growth from rent increases can outpace insurance escalation. In most coastal markets through 2026, that race is too close to bet confidently on either outcome.

The Airbnb Effect on Housing Markets Is Measurable and Larger Than Claimed

Short-term rental concentration in specific neighborhoods removes housing supply, raises long-term rents by measurable margins, and generates political backlash — but the regulatory response is inconsistent and often poorly targeted.

STR Market Data

▸ US active STR listings: ~1.3 million (AirDNA, 2025) — concentrated in 20-30 metros and resort markets

▸ Academic finding: 10% increase in Airbnb listings → 0.42% increase in rents, 0.76% increase in home prices (Barron, Kung & Proserpio, 2021)

▸ NYC STR regulations (Aug 2023): licensed listings dropped 98% within 60 days; hotel revenue increased 40% year-over-year

▸ Barcelona: banned new STR licenses 2024; estimated 10,000+ units returned to long-term market within 12 months

▸ New Orleans French Quarter: 35-40% of housing units estimated to be STR (pre-regulation)

The academic debate about whether Airbnb-type STRs materially affect housing costs has largely been resolved: they do, and the effect is concentrated in specific neighborhoods rather than distributed uniformly across metros. The Barron, Kung, and Proserpio paper — the most rigorous study to date, using Airbnb's own listing data matched to Census housing data — found that a 10% increase in STR listings corresponded to a 0.42% rent increase and a 0.76% home price increase in affected zip codes. These appear to be small numbers until you consider that in the most STR-concentrated neighborhoods — French Quarter New Orleans, Bernal Heights San Francisco, Williamsburg Brooklyn — STR penetration rates of 10-30% of housing stock imply rent effects of 4-12% above what would otherwise prevail. That is a material affordability impact for long-term residents.

New York City's 2023 regulatory intervention is the cleanest natural experiment on record. The city required STR operators to obtain a license and be present during guest stays — effectively banning the absentee investment model. Within 60 days, active Airbnb listings fell from approximately 22,000 to under 500. The housing market response was modest in aggregate (the city has 3.4 million housing units) but detectable in neighborhoods with high pre-regulation STR concentration. The more immediate effect was a 40% year-over-year increase in hotel revenue — suggesting a substantial portion of STR demand shifted to hotels rather than disappearing. For cities evaluating similar regulations, the NYC experience suggests the housing supply benefit is real but modest, while the hotel industry benefit is substantial. The political economy of STR regulation is shaped accordingly.

The Airbnb effect is neither as trivial as the platform's lobbyists claim nor as catastrophic as housing advocates suggest. In most metro housing markets, STRs are a small but nonzero contributor to affordability pressure. In specific resort towns, tourist neighborhoods, and urban entertainment districts, the effect is large enough to be the dominant driver of housing cost escalation. Regulation calibrated to the local concentration level — rather than blanket metro-wide bans or blanket permissiveness — is the analytically defensible approach.

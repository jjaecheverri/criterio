Zoning Reform Is the Supply-Side Variable Markets Are Underpricing

Economists estimate restrictive zoning adds 36% to housing costs in constrained markets; state-level reform in California, Oregon, and Montana demonstrates measurable supply response within 24-36 months of implementation.

▸ Estimated housing cost premium from restrictive zoning in coastal metros: 36% above competitive market price (Glaeser & Gyourko, updated 2024)

Zoning reform has moved from academic theory to measurable policy experiment in the last five years, and the early results are consequential enough to change how investors model supply risk in housing markets. The core insight — that restrictive single-family zoning in high-demand areas creates artificial scarcity that drives prices above competitive equilibrium — is not new. It dates to Glaeser and Gyourko's seminal 2002 work and has been replicated across dozens of metro-level studies. What is new is that several states have now run the experiment at scale, and the supply response, while not transformative on a 12-month horizon, is demonstrably positive on a 24-36 month horizon.

The California experience is instructive in both directions. ADU legalization under SB 9 and related bills has produced a genuine supply response — 25,000+ annual permits versus a pre-reform baseline of 5,000 — but the units are concentrated in wealthier neighborhoods where homeowners have the capital and motivation to build. The poorest neighborhoods with the greatest affordability needs have seen the smallest ADU uptake. This self-selection problem is not unique to California; it reflects the broader challenge that market-rate supply responses tend to be strongest where land values and household incomes are already highest. The reform is working, but it is partially misdirected. A more complete reform requires not just upzoning but infrastructure provision, financing mechanisms for affordable units, and removal of local permitting friction — all of which remain contested.

Real estate investors who model supply risk only through traditional permit data are missing the most important leading indicator in supply-constrained markets: zoning reform legislation. A state bill eliminating single-family-only zones is a more consequential supply signal than three months of permit data. Markets that have passed reform — California, Oregon, Montana, Washington, Vermont — deserve a structural discount to future rent growth projections relative to unreformed markets.

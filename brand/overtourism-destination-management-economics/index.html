Overtourism Is a Brand Management Problem

Destinations from Barcelona to Bali to Amsterdam face the paradox of tourism success destroying the product that attracted visitors — creating a destination brand management challenge with structural economic and political dimensions.

▸ Barcelona: 32M tourists annually to a city of 1.6M residents (20:1 ratio); resident anti-tourism protests 2024: 10,000+ participants

The overtourism problem is structurally different from ordinary demand management because tourism destinations are simultaneously economic assets (generating income for hotels, restaurants, tour operators, and government), political constituencies (residents who vote and whose quality of life is directly affected), and brand entities (whose attractiveness to future visitors depends on the experiential quality being preserved). These three interests are often in direct conflict: the economic interest is maximizing visitor volume; the political interest is limiting disruption to residents; and the brand interest is maintaining the authentic character that made the destination desirable. Managing the three simultaneously requires governance sophistication that most destination marketing organizations were not designed to provide.

Barcelona has become the most analytically interesting case study in overtourism governance. With 32 million annual tourists visiting a city of 1.6 million, the resident-to-tourist ratio creates unavoidable infrastructure stress: Gaudí sites require ticket reservations months in advance, the Gothic Quarter is impassable in peak season, housing costs have risen dramatically as STRs converted residential supply to tourist accommodation. The 2024 resident protests — which included activists squirting tourists with water pistols and posting signs declaring "tourism kills the neighborhood" — reflect genuine economic displacement: Barcelona's average apartment rental rose 68% between 2013 and 2023, and the municipal government's own research attributes 30-40% of that increase to the STR-driven conversion of housing stock. The city has implemented STR license moratoriums, tourist taxes, and visitor flow management at peak attractions — but the brand investment of decades of tourism marketing created a demand pipeline that is difficult to redirect.

Destination brand managers face a counter-intuitive strategic imperative: reducing demand for their destination in order to protect the product quality that sustains long-term brand value. The destinations that will succeed in the next decade are not those that maximize visitor volume, but those that maximize visitor value — charging more per visitor, distributing visitation across seasons and geographies, and actively managing the resident-tourist experience balance. The destinations that fail to make this transition will experience the Barcelona paradox: destroying the product through the success of selling it.

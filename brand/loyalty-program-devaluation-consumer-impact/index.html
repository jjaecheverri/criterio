Loyalty Program Devaluation Is a Hidden Brand Tax

Airlines, hotels, and credit card companies have systematically devalued loyalty currency over the past decade — reducing point values by 30-60%, inflating redemption costs, and creating a trust erosion with their most valuable customer segments.

Loyalty Program Devaluation Data

▸ United MileagePlus: domestic award rates increased 50-100% 2019-2024 on same routes (The Points Guy analysis)

▸ Marriott Bonvoy peak devaluation: points value fell from ~0.9 cents/pt to ~0.7 cents/pt (2019-2023) = 22% reduction

▸ American AAdvantage: eliminated fixed award chart 2023; dynamic pricing reduced published value by 30-50% for aspirational redemptions

▸ Delta SkyMiles: moved to revenue-based earning in 2015; eliminated fixed award chart; named "worst frequent flyer program" 2024 by multiple publications

▸ $700B+ in outstanding loyalty liability: airlines, hotels, credit cards; only 60-70% expected to be redeemed

Loyalty programs were designed as retention instruments — mechanisms to create switching costs and reward high-value customers through accumulated benefits. Over the past decade, the largest loyalty programs have systematically eroded the value of accumulated currency, converting what were once transparent, predictable redemption systems into dynamic pricing mechanisms that optimize for program economics at the expense of member value. The shift from fixed award charts to dynamic pricing at major airlines — American in 2023, Delta in 2015 — is the most visible manifestation of this trend: a round-trip business class redemption that previously cost 75,000 miles regardless of cash price might now cost 250,000 miles on a high-demand route during peak season. For members who accumulated miles under the old economics, this represents a retroactive devaluation of stored value.

The financial incentive for program devaluation is straightforward. An airline that has $7 billion in frequent flyer program liability on its balance sheet can reduce that liability — and improve its balance sheet — by inflating the number of miles required for redemption. The member's stored miles are worth less in purchasing power terms, but the balance sheet entry shrinks. This is financially logical but creates a trust problem with the most engaged members: the road warriors who fly 100,000 miles per year and have accumulated significant point balances are precisely the customers who notice devaluations, discuss them on enthusiast forums, and respond with reduced loyalty. The data on whether airline loyalty program devaluations have materially reduced elite member travel frequency is contested, but the anecdotal evidence from forums like FlyerTalk and View from the Wing suggests the correlation is real.

30-60%
Effective devaluation range for major airline loyalty program redemptions over the past decade

The brand implication of loyalty devaluation is that the highest-value customers — those with the most accumulated program engagement — have the strongest negative reaction to devaluation events. These are the customers with the highest lifetime value, the most influential social networks, and the most sophisticated ability to calculate and communicate the economics of the betrayal. Loyalty programs that have maintained or improved redemption value — Southwest Rapid Rewards, JetBlue TrueBlue — have earned measurably stronger NPS scores among their most loyal segments. The short-term balance sheet benefit of devaluation may be purchased at the cost of long-term loyalty among the most valuable customers.

Grocery Delivery Has a Permanent Profitability Problem

Unit economics for grocery delivery remain deeply negative for most operators even after a decade of optimization — the fundamental cost structure of rapid, ambient-and-cold last-mile delivery cannot be reconciled with the price elasticity of grocery consumers.

Grocery Delivery Economics

▸ Average order value (grocery delivery): $80-110; delivery cost (pick, pack, deliver): $15-25/order

▸ Implied gross margin required to break even: 17-25% on a category with 25-30% typical margins — leaves minimal contribution after labor and overhead

▸ Instacart reported profitability: reached adjusted EBITDA positive in 2023; GAAP profitability remains elusive

▸ Amazon Fresh UK: 7 of 19 warehouses closed 2023; US Fresh expansion: paused since 2022

▸ Gorillas, Getir, Jiffy: all exited or severely restructured US/European operations 2022-2024

▸ Walmart+ delivery: profitable in aggregate but cross-subsidized by membership economics, not delivery unit economics

The grocery delivery sector has spent approximately $15 billion in venture capital and $30+ billion in corporate investment since 2015 attempting to solve a unit economics problem that the underlying cost structure makes extremely difficult to resolve without either raising prices or reducing service quality. The cost of picking an order in a grocery store (or dedicated fulfillment center), packing it with appropriate cold chain handling, and delivering it within 2-4 hours to a residential address runs $15-25 per order at current labor costs — regardless of whether the operator is Instacart, DoorDash, Amazon, Walmart, or a local grocer. Against an average order value of $80-110 and a grocery gross margin of 25-30%, the contribution margin after delivery costs is razor-thin or negative before overhead, technology costs, and customer acquisition costs are allocated.

The business models that have shown the most resilience are those that have found structural subsidies for the delivery cost: Walmart+ subscribers who pay $98/year in exchange for "free" delivery (the membership fee subsidizes delivery economics); Amazon Prime members who receive similar bundled delivery; and Instacart's shift to a marketplace model where the delivery cost is partially shifted to markup on item prices. The 10-minute delivery model (dark stores / rapid-delivery) proved even more uneconomic than conventional grocery delivery — the economics required basket sizes above $30-35 to approach breakeven at a price point consumers rejected, leading to the sequential collapse of Gorillas, Getir, Gopuff, and their competitors in European and US markets. The survivors — Instacart, DoorDash, Uber Eats grocery — are profitable on an adjusted basis by managing delivery density and shifting economics toward restaurants or high-AOV categories rather than optimizing grocery delivery in isolation.

For grocery retailers evaluating delivery investment, the strategic question is not whether delivery is profitable in isolation — it isn't, for most operators — but whether it is an acceptable cost of membership in a competitive ecosystem where consumers now expect the option. The grocery chains that have threaded this needle effectively treat delivery as a loyalty and retention tool, not a profit center, and set delivery economics targets accordingly. The ones that have struggled tried to make delivery a standalone profit center and discovered the unit economics don't support it.

Dollar Store Saturation Is Reshaping Rural Retail: 38,000 Stores

Dollar General and Dollar Tree/Family Dollar have built a combined 38,000-store footprint that now serves as the primary grocery and household goods channel for millions of rural Americans — with measurable effects on local food access and competing retailers.

Dollar General has built the largest store network in American retail history by pursuing a simple thesis: rural and exurban America is underserved, price-sensitive, and lacks the income or transportation access to shop at Walmart or Amazon on a regular basis. With an average store of 7,400 square feet, a SKU count of 10,000-12,000, and a real estate cost structure that works in towns where no other national retailer will operate, Dollar General has become the de facto general store for communities that supermarket chains abandoned decades ago. In counties where Dollar General has achieved saturation — three or more stores per 10,000 residents — the company accounts for a measurable share of food spending despite selling minimal fresh produce.

The food access implications are the most contested dimension of dollar store saturation. The industry argues it is serving underserved communities that previously had no local retail option; critics argue it is actively displacing independent grocers and convenience stores that did carry more fresh food. The empirical evidence supports a nuanced version of both: in communities where Dollar General entered markets that had no prior grocery option, food access improved for residents without cars. In markets where its entry coincided with the closure of local IGA or independent grocery operators, the net effect on fresh food access was negative. The company's 2022 commitment to add fresh produce to 10,000 stores — 4,000 of which had been completed by 2025 — represents a recognition that the food desert critique was affecting its regulatory relationships, but the 8% current fresh produce penetration rate suggests the commitment is proceeding slowly.

Combined US dollar store locations — a retail footprint larger than McDonald's, Starbucks, and Walmart combined

For CPG companies, dollar stores are no longer a secondary channel — they are the primary route to market for 20-30 million rural Americans. The strategic implication is that pack formats, price points, and SKU architecture designed for Walmart or Kroger may not be optimal for the dollar store context. Companies that have invested in dollar store-specific formats — smaller pack sizes at lower absolute prices, private-label competitive sets, planogram discipline — are capturing disproportionate share in this channel. Those that treat dollar stores as a clearance outlet for slower-moving mainline SKUs are leaving significant volume on the table.
